Notachain: A Protocol for Legally-Anchored On-Chain Attestations

Working Paper — Draft for Discussion

Abstract

Notachain is a protocol that brings private judicial authority on-chain. By enabling credentialed legal professionals — notaries, attorneys, solicitors, barristers, and judicial officers — to register cryptographic keypairs on a public, immutable registry, Notachain creates a class of attestations backed by the full weight of institutional legal accountability.

These attestations are boolean: true or false. A credentialed professional verifies an off-chain fact, signs a minimum-disclosure attestation, and publishes it on-chain. The wallet holder's identity is never exposed. The attestation is owned by the wallet holder and can be consumed by any protocol that needs verified off-chain facts.

1. The Problem

1.1 The Missing Primitive

Oracles solved the problem of bringing market data on-chain. Price feeds, weather data, sports results — there is well-established infrastructure for external data that exists independently of any individual.

There is no equivalent for human data. There is no standardised way for a person to prove a fact about themselves on-chain without either revealing their identity entirely or trusting a centralised intermediary that becomes a single point of failure.

This blocks entire categories of on-chain products. Insurance underwriters cannot price individual risk because wallets are pseudonymous. Lending protocols cannot offer undercollateralised loans because creditworthiness cannot be verified. Age-gating, credential verification, and professional licensing all require off-chain facts that the chain cannot discover on its own.

1.2 The Gödel Constraint

This is not a feature gap. It is a logical constraint. A system cannot verify statements about itself from within itself. A blockchain can record what wallets did. It cannot verify who controls them or what they do off-chain. At some point, an external input is required — and that input must come from someone accountable.

1.3 The On-Chain Transparency Advantage

Blockchain is often framed as a privacy liability. For verification, it is the opposite. On-chain transaction history is perfectly recorded, immutable, and publicly verifiable. Every approval, every interaction, every destination address is timestamped and permanent.

This transparency creates a forensic foundation that traditional systems cannot match. The remaining challenge is not verifying what happened on-chain — it is verifying the off-chain facts that precede and contextualise on-chain events.

2. The Boolean Attestation

2.1 Structure

Every Notachain attestation reduces to a boolean. Not "this person earns $85,000" but "this person meets the income threshold." True or false. Signed by a credentialed professional. The proof document lives on IPFS — self-contained and content-addressed. Only the hash and the signature go on-chain.

2.2 Monotonic and Temporal Booleans

Some booleans, once true, are true forever. A date of birth threshold cannot become false. A historical fact — "this person held citizenship on this date" — is permanently true at its timestamp regardless of what happens later. These are notarised once and remain valid for the lifetime of the wallet.

Other booleans are temporal — they may change over time. Employment status, security practices, claim history, educational credentials. These require periodic re-attestation. The re-attestation cadence is not set by the protocol — it is determined by whatever consuming protocol requires the data. An insurer may require annual re-attestation of security practices. A lender may require quarterly income verification.

2.3 Two Legal Traditions, Two Attestation Anatomies

In civil law jurisdictions — continental Europe, Latin America, East Asia, and much of Africa — the notary owns the attestation. They are not witnessing someone else's declaration. They are making their own. A French notaire or German Notar is appointed by the state, often for life. Their authenticated instruments carry prima facie evidentiary weight in court and are enforceable without further proceedings. Their liability is total and undivided.

In common law jurisdictions — the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and others — the legal professional is the conduit, not the testator. The wallet holder makes the declaration. The attorney, solicitor, barrister, or notary witnesses it — verifying identity, confirming the declaration was made voluntarily and knowingly. The truth of the underlying claim belongs to the wallet holder. The professional's liability is for knowingly facilitating a false one, which is a criminal offence.

The attestation structure is identical. The legal accountability underneath is different. This distinction is reflected in the Notachain trust tier system.

3. Protocol Architecture

3.1 Overview

Notachain operates as a set of smart contracts on an existing EVM-compatible chain. No new chain. No new consensus mechanism. The protocol needs immutability, public auditability, and smart contract execution — all of which an existing EVM provides.

The protocol comprises three core components: the Registry, the Attestation Layer (request-response architecture), and proof documents stored on IPFS.

3.2 The Registry

The registry is a public, immutable on-chain record mapping credentialed legal professional public keys to their credentials: jurisdiction, licensing body, contact details, registration timestamp, and trust tier classification.

Who qualifies

Notachain uses the term "attestor" broadly to mean any credentialed legal professional with attestation authority, professional accountability, and legal liability for fraudulent conduct. This includes civil law notaries, attorneys, solicitors, barristers, common law notaries public, and judicial officers. What qualifies a participant is not the job title but the accountability structure: a recognised licensing body with real disciplinary power, personal legal liability, and publicly verifiable credentials.

Self-registration

Individual professionals self-register on the Notachain registry. They generate an asymmetric keypair, retaining the private key under exclusive physical control, and submit their public key alongside verifiable credential metadata to the registry contract. They declare their licensing body, jurisdiction, and professional credentials.

The registry does not verify credentials itself. Trust is established through two orthogonal mechanisms: cryptographic (the signature is unforgeable without the private key) and legal (impersonating a licensed professional is a criminal offence in every jurisdiction). Out-of-band verification is always available — anyone can confirm an attestor's credentials by checking the public register of their licensing body.

Recognised licensing bodies

The protocol maintains a reference list of recognised licensing bodies. This includes all civil law notariats that are members of the International Union of Notaries (UINL) — 92 member notariats representing approximately 70% of the global population — and approved common law professional bodies such as bar associations, law societies, and notarial societies.

This list is initially maintained by the Notachain protocol entity. It is published on-chain, fully auditable, and subject to progressive decentralisation through the Notachain DAO as institutional participants join the governance structure.

The protocol entity

The protocol entity serves as the actor of last resort. At launch, it maintains the reference list of recognised licensing bodies, handles key rotation requests, manages operational decisions, and processes emergency actions. Individual professionals do not need to wait for their licensing body to participate in order to register — the protocol entity verifies their credentials directly.

As the network matures and institutional participants — licensing bodies, insurance companies, and other stakeholders — join the DAO through token grants, governance authority progressively migrates from the entity to the DAO. The entity's role diminishes over time by design.

Key rotation

In the event an attestor loses access to their signing keypair, rotation requires identity verification through the appropriate formal process recognised by their jurisdiction and licensing body. The protocol entity processes rotation requests as the actor of last resort. Where a licensing body has registered on-chain as a DAO participant, it may take over rotation authority for its own members.

The security model for rotation is consistent with the security model for registration: institutional legitimacy verified through real-world processes that an international attacker cannot easily subvert.

DAO governance

The long-term governance model is progressive decentralisation. The DAO grant pool incentivises institutional participation. When a licensing body registers on-chain, it receives a token grant. The individual who facilitated that registration receives a referral reward. This creates a network effect where participants are economically incentivised to recruit institutions, and institutions are economically incentivised to join.

Over time, the DAO — composed of licensing bodies, insurance companies, and other institutional stakeholders — governs the reference list of recognised bodies, protocol parameters, and treasury releases. The protocol entity retains operational authority for time-sensitive decisions but is accountable to the DAO through the on-chain audit trail.

3.3 The Attestation Layer

Request-response architecture

The attestation layer is a request-response protocol on an immutable ledger. Someone needs a fact attested. They make a request. A credentialed professional verifies the fact and signs a response. Both are on-chain, permanent, timestamped, and cryptographically tied to a specific professional whose real-world identity and accountability structure is recorded in the registry.

The attestation in practice

A wallet holder presents evidence to a registered professional. The professional verifies the fact and produces a formal attestation — whether as their own notarial instrument (civil law) or as a witnessed declaration (common law), depending on their jurisdiction and legal tradition. The proof document is published to IPFS. Only the hash and signature go on-chain. The wallet holder's identity is never on-chain.

Legal accountability as trust amplifier

A legal professional who fraudulently certifies false information risks criminal prosecution, loss of professional license, and civil liability. For civil law notaries, corrupting a notarial instrument is corruption of a state-delegated judicial function. Criminal, civil, and disciplinary proceedings run simultaneously. Personal liability is unlimited.

The trust in a Notachain attestation derives from two independent, reinforcing sources: cryptographic unforgeability and legal deterrence.

3.4 Trust Tiers

Tier Category Trust weight
Tier A UINL civil law notaries Highest. Quasi-judicial status. Prima facie evidentiary weight. Suitable for high-value applications.
Tier B Common law lawyer-attestors (US attorneys, UK solicitors and barristers, AU solicitors, NZ, SG, HK, Quebec notaires) High. Licensed legal practitioners with professional liability, disciplinary oversight, and criminal exposure.
Tier C Administrative notaries (US notaries public, general commissioners) Moderate. Non-lawyer officials with limited attestation authority. May require multiple attestations for high-value applications.

4. Trust Model and Attack Surface

4.1 What Notachain trusts

No system is trustless. Notachain is explicit about its trust assumptions: standard cryptographic primitives, attestor honesty backed by legal liability rather than cryptographic enforcement, and registry integrity mitigated by on-chain auditability and immutability.

4.2 Attack vectors

Fraudulent self-registration: mitigated by criminal liability for impersonating a licensed professional, out-of-band verification via public licensing body registers, and the ability to require multiple independent attestations for high-value applications.

Key compromise: mitigated by physical security of signing material and key rotation through formal identity verification processes that an international attacker cannot easily subvert.

Attestor collusion: mitigated by criminal fraud liability, professional disciplinary consequences, multi-attestor requirements for high-value applications, and cross-jurisdictional panel requirements in the dispute resolution process.

Reference list manipulation: mitigated by on-chain auditability, community ability to fork, progressive decentralisation of list governance through the DAO, and the fact that recognised bodies are publicly verifiable institutions that cannot be invented.

5. Applications

The attestation layer is general-purpose. Any protocol that requires verified off-chain facts can consume Notachain attestations as a trust primitive. Initial applications include:

  • Insurance: underwriters can price individual risk using attested off-chain facts (security practices, claim history, hardware usage) combined with on-chain behavioral data. Claims are adjudicated by reading the ledger, not arguing about what happened.
  • Lending: undercollateralised loans become possible when income or creditworthiness can be attested without revealing the underlying data.
  • Identity: age-gating, credential verification, and professional licensing without centralised KYC.
  • Procurement: verifying counterparty credentials, jurisdictional compliance, and professional qualifications.

6. Open Problems

  • Cross-jurisdictional enforcement: jurisdiction follows the attestation instrument, but this requires legal validation across target markets.
  • Attestation tooling: the signing and publishing process must be simple enough for a legal professional with no cryptographic knowledge. This middleware layer needs its own design.
  • Dynamic attestations: a mechanism for periodic re-attestation of temporal booleans and for consuming protocols to check attestation freshness.
  • Privacy enhancement: zero-knowledge proofs may be layered on top of the attestation primitive to enable verification of attestation properties without revealing the attestation contents. This is a future enhancement, not a launch requirement.
  • Regulatory framework: the legal classification of on-chain attestations and the licensing requirements for products that consume them are open questions requiring regulatory dialogue.
  • Chain selection: the specific EVM-compatible chain for deployment based on fee economics and security requirements.

7. Conclusion

Notachain proposes a synthesis of centuries-old legal institutional infrastructure and modern cryptographic trust systems. The civil law notariat, representing 70% of the global population through the UINL's 92 member notariats, provides an immediate global foundation of private judicial authority. A French notaire's attestation is not a professional declaration — it is a judicial act. Bringing that on-chain is not incremental. It is qualitative.

The insight at the core of Notachain is simple: blockchain does not hide. Every event is recorded. Every transaction is permanent. But blockchain cannot see beyond itself. A system cannot verify statements about itself from within itself. It needs an external input — accountable, verifiable, and legally enforceable — to know anything about the people behind the wallets.

Notachain is not a chain. It is the missing trust layer between the chain and the world.
